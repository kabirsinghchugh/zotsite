Brief Communication
Modelling the communication, distribution, transmission or transfer of scientific informationYves F. Le Coadic
Conservatoire National des Arts et Metiers (CNAM), Paris; and Ministry of Education (DBMIST), Paris. France
Received 22 October 1986
The processes involved in distributing scientific information, i.e. information produced by and for the sciences, assume various names: communication, diffusion, transmission, transfer, flow, spread, circulation, dissemination, etc.
Depending on the conceptual point of view, the distribution processes are patterned in different ways. As physics is currently the leading scientific field, physical science models are often used. Two of them are studied here and their limitations are shown.
But biology and biological models have also attracted certain information scientists, and again we show the limitations to these.
All of these processes are, however, of a social kind, which indicates that only sociological models, and particularly those of mathematical sociology are appropriate in studying this phenomena. Various models are discussed here and a new conceptual approach is proposed.
Terminology
It is difficult not to be surprised when leafing through the many writings on the distribution of scientific and technical information, when we hear it spoken of by some as transfer, by others as diffusion, while some speak of transmission and, still others as communication, dissemination or circulation. Sometimes, even within the same document, several of these words are used without any really apparent reason.
This leads one to worry about the grounds of discussion in information science. The language used is more an ordinary everyday one than scientific, and the worry arises from the manifold ex-

But the difficulties involved are not only terminological. In fact, they reveal the incredible mess of conceptual and epistemological difficulties that afflicts the current pre-scientific state of information science. An example of confusion at the conceptual level giving rise to this multitude of names is that of considering the ‘information’ concept of the theory of ‘information’ to be analogous to the concept of information in the human communication process. If the phenomena studied were the same in both cases, we should have an unequivocal meaning characteristic of any scientific concept, defined by the researchers to cover a definite set of phenomena and relations. If we have to deal with a plurality of concepts, it is because the phenomena studied are not identical. Analogy
First let us take a look at the methods that have been proposed for constructing these models. They are quite often based on analogy, an interdisciplinary approach that is supposed to make it easier to transfer ideas from one field to another, but which can also obstruct progress in the knowledge of a phenomenon for a long time, as we shall see below [5], In these times, when pseudo-scientific disguises crop up all the time and in all fields of mental and material life the recourse to models from the ‘exact’ sciences often involves deception or tricks, lubricated by visible or obvious analogies. One of the most noteworthy tricks is, for example, recourse to the physical concept of entropy in the study of the human communication process [6,13], and also of other non-physical processes. The use of an analogy does not require any real isomorphism between the compared structures, and may be dangerous when a mechanical language is applied systematically to social phenomena, or when we attempt to describe these phenomena in terms of ethology [7].
This is not to say that analogy cannot be used. It helps to fit things together and stimulates innovation. For example, there is no reason why possible formal isomorphisms should not be investigated between physical and social distributions. The existence of large disparities between the entities in question is no reason why a general theory should not be sought out. After all, as Rapoport says, the temperature and the density of a gas are two very' different things: yet they are governed by the same differential equations [8].
The natural tendency is thus to extend the domain of the successful theory, even if this means shoving ideas around and introducing “arbitrary parallels” [9]. There are many examples - gravitation, planetary model, entropy, etc. - where concepts and language are borrowed from other fields. And these other fields are just the ones where we use quantitative scientific concepts or quantitative methods to “express laws in the form of mathematical functions by which predictions can be formulated in the most precise and efficient way” [10], Although there is no question of ignoring qualitative concepts or wanting to “set up ‘mathematicity’ as a general criterion of ‘scien- tificity* ” [3] and thereby eliminating the sciences of taxonomy (botany, zoology), there is no question cither of settling for statistical analysis, as is still often true in the science of information (such as the bibliometric laws), with its jungle of data covering up an underlying desert of concepts [11].
Formalisms
The process ordinarily used for maintaining and extending a successful theory is to take its formulae and apply them to new fields where they denote new concepts, generalizing those that carry the known laws [9]. This is the case for theories using the concept of potential. We will see an example of this below. This method of formal extension has met with considerable success in quantum physics. The form of the equations of classical mechanics is conserved while the interpretation and even the mathematical nature of the symbols in the equations are changed: in place of numbers there are matrices.
Structures
But these attractive formal theories ignore any hypothesis of structure among the objects in their formulae, as opposed to the structural theories where objects are emphasized rather than relations, or the concepts supporting the laws rather than the form of the laws. Yet elements having the same structure may exhibit analogous properties. Such an analogy has already suggested unsuspected properties for one of the two fields involved in the transfer-by-analogy: the core-drop structure Bohr conjured up to explain atomic structure by analogy with a drop of water (the elements of the core structure being the nucleons and the elements of the drop structure being the molecules) translated the known laws of evaporation into the laws of nuclear fission.
The formulae and structures that have been - and still are - called upon the most are those of physics, the “both experimental and mathema- tized science that offers the ideal dialectic model between practice and theory that any science
worthy of the name should obey” [3], Information science has thus sought to copy its method from that of physics. This has been the general trend for some 30 years, with the non-physical sciences adopting the scientific method that was previously monopolized by physics. This is not a ‘physicali- zation’ of science, though. It does not mean giving up the study of the non-physical processes, or attempting to reduce non-physical processes to physical ones, but rather of studying them scientifically and in depth [12],
Let us look at a few examples of this modelling.
First model: the physical concept of diffusion of information
This model refers explicitly to physics. The diffusion processes are dominant processes in the physical sciences, whether it be in physics itself (with the diffusion of light and, more generally, of electromagnetic radiation, but also the diffusion of heat) or in chemistry (molecular or ionic diffusion, diffusion in solids, etc.)
The formal theory which has undergone a certain development was proposed in 1973 by Avramescu [14], who considers that the diffusion of scientific and technical information is analogous to that of heat in solids, and applies the Fourier law to the information. What can we say about this model? That it induces a strong ‘ physicalistic’ determination and thereby eliminates the social aspects of diffusion. It applies only to writings and ignores oral communication. It says nothing about the number of researchers or persons who have received the information, which is still the purpose of the process if we want to consider it as a process of social communication. More fundamentally, the theories of physical diffusion are conservative theories, i.e. the quantity of energy diffused during the process is conserved. This is not true for social diffusioninformation does not obey a law of conservation. If you have one Franc and I have one Franc and we exchange them, we still each have one Franc. On the other hand, if you have one idea and I have one too (but different), and we exchange them, we then each have two ideas. Finally, this model of diffusion adopts the hypotheses of a strictly directional transmission (the choice of the term is not neutral), thus borrowing from the physical conceptualization form and from the cur-rently dominant physical model in information science, which we will now analyse.

This relates an emitter E, which ‘communicates’ a message M, to the receiver R. The ideas of code, channel, noise and feedback are brought in to refine this idea, but without making it into a model of social communication.
The careless use that has been made of this analogy has, we believe, hindered progress in the knowledge of the circulation of information. Can it not be thought that, in the same way as the analogy of irrigation in physiology long obstructed progress in the knowledge of how the blood circulates, as discovered by Harvey, the signal transmission analogy has impeded progress in our knowledge of the communication of information? Adding to the conceptual difficulties like the confusion already pointed out between the ‘information’ concept in electronic communication and its counterpart in human communication, this analogy sets up an epistemological obstacle to the science of information. We thus can only conclude that the convenient technical concept of transmission of transfer have to be dropped.

Conclusion
Thus dawns the reality of the sociological concept of communication which presupposes, among other things as we have pointed out above, the dropping of the physical concepts and in particular the highly deceptive technical concept of transmission. This also assumes the abandonment of the heterogeneous linear structure originating this concept, and its replacement with a homogeneous circular structure where the social actors of the process appear, instead of technical devices:
